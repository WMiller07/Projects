For the remainder of this presentation, all trends will be indexed to the same starting point for the sake of comparison. When looking at indexed trends, it is helpful to remember that percent changes are larger in amount for larger metrics: a change of $300,000 to income is comparatively smaller than a $300,000 change to new goods purchases.

At this scale, fluctuations in gross profit, income, and purchases all look very small; however, our bottom line is greatly affected by these fluctuations, and it's worth looking at them in detail.

These metrics, and all that follow, are from stores that are currently open and were open prior to 1/1/2010.


A more detailed look at our gross profit shows that we recently reached a long-term high of $11 million in comparative-store gross profit in September of 2018. (This also held true for HPB as a whole.) This is an increase of $220,000 from 1/1/2011.

Both income and gross profit dropped dramatically for comparative locations from 1/1/2011 until summer of 2014, when iStore was rolled out chain-wide. This strongly contributed to chain-wide recovery of income and gross profit. However, income has since remained flat as gross profit has continued to increase.

Money spent on used goods has continuously declined in this time period, along a trend that does not correlate with either income of gross profit.

Money spent on new goods has varied significantly during this time period in manner that correlates strongly with gross profit. As new purchases increased in early 2014, gross profit decreased along a similar trend. As new goods purchases decreased beginning in summer of 2014, gross profit increased, which was also fueled by the introduction of iStore. Since early 2017, new goods purchases have continued to decline. This appears to correlate with a slight drop in income, but with a large increase in gross profit that closely correlates with it.


One of the biggest factors thought to affect income during this time period is a decline sales transaction count. This decline has been quite steep, with our number of sales transactions at comparative locations being down 25% from 1/1/2011; however, our income at these locations in only down 2.76% during this time period.

As our number of transactions has declined, the amount of our average sale has gone up to compensate, and the amount of this increase has accelerated in the past 2 years - as can be seen from the curvature of the "Avg Sale Amount" line.

As shown in the previous chart, gross profit has increased, so the loss of transactions has not negatively affected gross profit.


During 2014, the percentage of transactions which were composed mostly of distribution items increased dramatically, causing a corresponding decrease in transactions which were mostly composed of used items. Since used items almost always have a lower cost than new items, this corresponded with a decline in gross profit.

Since 2014, transactions which were primarily made up of distribution have declined faster than our overall decline in transaction count, and transactions made up primarily of used items have begun to even out. Transactions made up of 75%-100% used items have begun to increase in recent months.

The steeper decline of transactions which are mostly new, combined with the levelling off of transactions which are mostly used, has correlated with a strong increase in gross profit.


The quantity of used items purchased has continuously decreased. While it's possible that the decreases in used quantity purchased and sales transaction countare related , the decreases do not correlate. Note how steep the drop in used quantity purchased was in 2011 to 2015 compared to sales transactions (25% drop in used quantity compared to 11% in sales transactions), whereas recently used quantity purchased has begun to flatten out as sales transaction count continues to decline.

The decline in new quantity received follows the exact same trend as the decline in sales transaction count from 2011 through then end of 2016. Beginning in 2017, new quantity drops off much more sharply. Sales transaction count also drops somewhat more sharply during this time period, though not to the same degree as sales transaction count.


Looking at inventory available on the sales floor during this time period, it is clear that the decline in new goods received resulted in a large decline in new quantity available.

The decline in used purchases did not result in a decline in used merchandise on the sales floor, however. Used quantity available on the sales floor increased dramatically, which correlated very closely with the increases in gross profit. The increase in used merchandise on the sales floor during 2017 arrested a decline in both income and gross profit, causing a income to even out and slightly increase, and causing gross profit to increase dramatically.

While used merchandise available did increase dramatically, it did not increase quite enough to offset the decrease in new merchandise in the past year. At beginning of 2018, used quantity on sales floor was up 720k items and new quantity was down 857k. At the beginning of 2019 used quantity was up 722k items and new quantity was down by 1.76 million items. It is likely that the decline in new quantity received was too sharp and dramatic to be compensated for by used merchandise, but income stayed flat during this time period, regardless.


Looking closer into factors affecting used quantity available durign this same time period, it's apparent that the amount of used merchandise not on the sales floor was it its peak during the inventory during the summer of 2017, when we had 1.17 million more used items in backstock than January of 2015, which is nearly 300k higher than any other time period, and is more than double any amount of back stock we've had since.

Comparing backstock to gross profit, it appears that gross profit greatly benefited from this backstock in the long term. As this back stock was decreased between July of 2017 and January 2018, used quantity availabe on the sales floor dramatically increased, and gross profit increased along with it. With the absence of such significant back stock in July of 2018, used quantity slightly decreased, and gross profit decreased along with it.

As seen in a previous chart, used purchases were declining dramatically during this time, even as our used quantities on hand were reaching all-time highs.


(Note: the data in this chart is not annualized so that the periodic nature of our pricing is clear.) At first glance, it appears as if the quantity of used items purchased during this time period was also declining. Quantity purchased in July of 2014 was 4.47 million, while used quantity purchased in July of 2018 was 3.75 million - a drop of over 16%.

Our number of items SIPS'd has continously increased during this time period, recently eclipsing used quantity purchased in some months. While at first it might seem like this could be due to increasing adoption of SIPS combined with catching up on quantity, the lowest percentage of hand-priced inventory of "SIPS'able" item types (everything that is not a comic, magazine, cassette tape, or VHS) was 15%, which was counted in the June 2019 inventory. In January of 2019, this percentage was nearly 19%.


Adjusting our quantity of items priced to account for 15% which was not SIPS'd, it is apparent that between SIPS and hand-pricing items we've been pricing a significantly higher quantity than we've been entering at the buy counter, and catching up on pricing items purchased during peak buy season is insufficient to account for this.

On investigation, this seems to be due to adoption of the "Garrison method" of accounting for buy quantities at a large number of locations. However, a large number of items that were not paid for are still being priced and product on the sales floor.

It has turned out that this method of account for used quantities purchased has exacerbated inventory adjustments rather than alleviated them. Used items unaccounted for, yet put on the sales floor, cause unpredictable fluctations in both average used item costs and used quantity on sales floor. Our true used cost of goods becomes impossible to calculate. It is the offical recommendation of operations department that all used items intended to be kept for sale are entered into the buy module. When a $0.00 cost is implemented is up to the discretion of DMs, but any quantity of items purchased should be accounted for.


With new quantity on sales floor decreasing dramatically in recent years, and with shifting market factors affecting item types such as paperbacks, it's interesting to look at how the product mix on the sales floor has changed chain-wide.

The most dramatic change has been the decrease in new hardbacks, which makes up 1.2 million of the 1.63 million item decrease in new goods on sales floor. All categories of new goods have decreased significantly, however, with new sidelines and new audio also decreasing by large amounts.

Used hardbacks have increased to partially offset the decrease in new hardbacks, increasing in quantity on sales floor more than any other used item type. Used video (almost entirely DVDs) has increased, as well, making up 214k of the increase in used item count. The count of periodicals has increased significantly as well, made up primarily of comics. The count of collectible items has dropped in the past year, but this is likely related to the adoption of the full-scan inventory method. Paperbacks have decreased by 330k items since the beginning of 2015. The increase in DVDs and decrease in paperbacks corresponds with the recommendations made as part of the section surveys done in recent years, and represents a much more profitable profit mix - with space devoted to DVDs generally being much more profitable than PBs.

Overall, total quantity on sales floor at comparative locations is down 700k items. Increases in used goods on sales floor has historically correlated with increased gross profit, and anything that can be done to fill any empty space with used merchandise is very likely to cause continued increase in gross profit.

  • (For the sake of planning for the future and accurate accounting for our physical inventories, all used goods received that are intended for sale should have their quantities entered into the buy module.)

  • Gross profit has recently reached a long-term high. This is primarily due to decreases in new goods purchases, with used goods replacing new on the sales floor.

  • While the increase in gross profit has been significant, it has been limited by failure to fully replace the quantity of new goods with used on the sales floor.

  • The largest increases in gross profit were from July of 2017 to July of 2018, when large quantities of used back stock were pushed to the sales floor as new goods received decreased. Since then, used back stock available has been insufficient to offset the drop in new merchandise, though gross profit has remained at a several year high.
